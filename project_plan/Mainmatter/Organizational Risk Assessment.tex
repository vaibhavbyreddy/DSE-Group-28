\chapter{Project Risk Assessment \& Management}

\section{Project Risk Assessment}   \label{sec:risk_assessment}
Based on a group discussion, seven key risks were identified regarding the organisation and management of the project. These included: cloud service failure, encountering a bottleneck in the design process, miscommunication between team members, external/internal advice delay, poor time estimation, interpersonal conflict and absence of team members. A likelihood score and a severity score were assigned to each risk based on its possible influence on the project. The resulting risk map can be seen in \autoref{fig:risk_matrix}.

    \centering

    \caption{Project Risk Map}
    \label{fig:risk_matrix}
\end{figure}

Based on the risk analysis, three crucial project risks were identified. Miscommunication between team members was estimated as very likely to occur, with a possibly catastrophic impact if not acted upon immediately. A design bottleneck (scenario in which a delay in one task disrupts the flow of multiple other tasks or parts of the project) was also identified as a risk of potentially catastrophic results, with a high possibility of occurrence (especially if combined with miscommunication). Additionally, cloud service failure was chosen as a key risk based on the experience of previous year's teams. Therefore, it was decided that risk management plans shall be drafted for each of the aforementioned scenarios.

\section{Risk Management Plans} \label{sec:risk_management}
A risk management plan shall incorporate both preventative measures and contingency plans to reduce both the likelihood and impact severity for each scenario. In this Section, such plans are developed for each of the crucial project risks identified in \autoref{sec:risk_assessment}.

\subsection{Miscommunication}
Miscommunication between team members is, by far, the most severe and common risk for an engineering project. If not addressed and identified soon enough, it can have catastrophic consequences. Examples of miscommunication scenarios include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A team member performing their tasks with no communication with the rest of the team, then discovering that their results do not match the outputs of the rest of the group.
    \item Several subteams developing parts of the aircraft independently, then realizing that their outputs are not compatible.
    \item A team member missing a session without prior notice, disrupting the flow and schedule of the project etc.
\end{itemize}

Common preventative measures for miscommunication include: active listening, using clear and concise language, immediately addressing any points of uncertainty, asking for feedback and making use of project planning tools and logic diagrams to clearly define lines of responsibility. Additionally, keeping track of the progress of each team member can be done by morning and afternoon debriefs during each work session.

Once miscommunication is identified, the most important contingency steps include: a thorough analysis of the severity and extent of the impacts resulting from miscommunication, identification of the source and correcting it, confirming that all sides have understood the reasons behind the misunderstanding and the steps required to correct it, and a review of the mitigation steps developed by the team.


\subsection{Bottleneck}
A bottleneck in the design process occurs when a single task getting delayed inhibits the development of several other aspects of the project, essentially blocking the work flow of multiple team members. Bottlenecks typically stem from poor project planning, and can significantly hinder the development of a project if not addressed immediately.

Preventative measures for bottlenecks are mostly centred around project planning, scheduling, and early identification of actions which may become congestion points for the project. To this end, the team shall make use of the WFD, WBS, Gantt Chart and the organogram, and ensure that sufficient resources are delegated to the crucial tasks.

Once a bottleneck occurs, it can be resolved by prioritizing the design steps causing the congestion in the process. Commonly used techniques to achieve this include: redirecting additional team members (particularly those whose work is blocked by the bottleneck), redirecting part of the work to additional research that can provide solutions to the problem, as well as reaching out to project mentors or external experts with relevant experience.

\subsection{Cloud Service Failure}
Cloud service failure is highly relevant for a team project. Possible scenarios include server failure, which happened to the teams in 2025, and network congestion, which is not uncommon at the TU Delft facilities.

No preventative measures can be taken to ensure that cloud services do not fail, since their operation is dependent on an external provider.


In case cloud failure occurs, the team shall make use of an offline text editor that will have the latest version of the project saved. The majority of the online editors are Cloudflare-based. Hence, an external work environment is needed. The project was set to be automatically saved to a Git Hub account, so extraction of the base report is doable all the time as long as an internet connection is available. As an offline text editor, VSCode shall be used.
