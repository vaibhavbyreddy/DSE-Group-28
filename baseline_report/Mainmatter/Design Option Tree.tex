\chapter{Preliminary Configuration Development}

This chapter details a systematic, top-down approach employed to develop design concepts for a high-speed aeroelastic testing platform. The approach employed utilizes Design Option Trees (DOT) to present possible solutions, shown in \autoref{fig: DOT}. First, top-level architectural designs are presented in \autoref{sec:platform_selection}, to later determine the vehicle category best suited to achieve the project objectives whilst adhering to the requirements specified in \autoref{tab:system_requirements}. Following the selection of a vehicle category, a second DOT is used to explore subsystem options. Finally, preliminary configuration concepts are generated in \autoref{sec:prelim_config_concepts}, yielding a shortlist of viable strawman design concepts to be further analysed in the detailed trade-off phase.

\section{Top-Level Platform Selection}
\label{sec:platform_selection}
Before evaluating specific aircraft subsystems, an initial design option tree was generated to determine the overarching vehicle architecture. This top-level tree functions as an "OR Tree" where the primary path is selected from distinct, mutually exclusive operational concepts. Three main approaches were evaluated to perform aeroelastic experiments mid-flight: a replaceable planform on a dedicated drone, a per-test custom-made drone, and designing an invariant aircraft capable of mounting test specimens. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Replaceable planform:} Accepted for further development due to high versatility. In relation to the alternative platforms, one with a replaceable planform was selected for further conceptualisation as it was immediately evident that it would allow for the widest variation of experiments.
    \item \textbf{Test-specific custom design:} Rejected due to existence of preferable alternatives. A test-specific custom design was considered as it would represent less structural complexity in relation to a fully replaceable planform. The concept was not further developed as it was deemed that generating a new design and manufacturing iteration, and possibly re-certifying the aircraft for each experiment, was significantly more complex and wasteful than overcoming structural complexities.
    \item \textbf{Mounting on invariant aircraft:} Rejected due to existence of preferable alternatives. An invariant aircraft with mounting ports was suggested as an option to carry out aeroelastic experiments. Benefits considered included a relative simplicity stemming from the invariant planform, but the concept was ultimately not developed further as it was deemed that it was too restrictive to the range of experiments that could be carried out upon it, in relation to the varying planform alternative.
\end{itemize}

For each of these top-level approaches, various vehicle types were considered, including fixed-wing aircraft, copters (both helicopters and multicopters), Vertical Take-Off and Landing (VTOL) fixed-wing aircraft, and towing aircraft.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rotary-Wing Platforms (Helicopters \& Multicopters):} Rejected across all branches due to requirement violations. Rotary wing platforms were proposed due to "tried and true" excellence in modularity and payload flexibility, yet their style of flight makes high-speed subsonic flight unfeasible, directly in violation of requirement (HUG-OPS-005) \cite{He2026}.
    \item \textbf{VTOL Fixed-Wing:} Rejected across all branches due to mass and complexity penalties. This concept was proposed to increase operational flexibility by increasing the number of suitable take-off and landing locations. However, in addition to increasing design complexity, the addition of a tilting mechanism for the engines would rapidly consume the tight 50kg mass budget (HUG-GEN-001).\cite{raymer1992aircraft}.
    \item \textbf{Towing Aircraft:} Rejected as an invariant platform due to requirement violations. This concept aimed to reduce the mass of the flying laboratory by removing its independent thrust components, and instead obtaining thrust from a separate plane which tows it. The concept was initially rejected due to the expected disturbance of the tow plane's wake to the airflow seen by the test article (HUG-AER-004) \cite{Vos}. Furthermore, it was deemed that this concept would risk the tow plane during flutter testing it if became uncontrollable which would violate Specific Operations Risk Assessment (SORA) range safety limits (HUG-CER-001, HUG-CER-002) \cite{livne}.
    \item \textbf{Conventional Fixed-Wing:} Accepted across all branches. This concept was deemed as the only viable architecture capable of achieving Mach 0.75, whilst maintaining clean airflow for test specimens (HUG-OPS-005, HUG-AER-001). Furthermore, a conventional fixed-wing drone was most conducive to predictable, decoupled flight dynamics necessary for baseline flutter suppression testing (HUG-CTL-001, HUG-STR-005) \cite{raymer1992aircraft, Vos, Abramov2014}.
\end{itemize}

From this analysis, the drone architecture was narrowed down to a conventional fixed-wing design. 

\section{Subsystem Design Option Tree}
\label{sec:design_option_tree}
Having established a conventional fixed-wing approach, a second detailed DOT was generated. Unlike the top-level selection, this fixed-wing tree acts as an "AND Tree," where valid configurations are built by combining accepted options from each fundamental subsystem group (Fuselage, Empennage, Wing, Propulsion, and Undercarriage). 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rejected Concepts (Red):} Options eliminated by unanimous group consensus. Rejections were strictly based on obvious technical infeasibility or direct conflicts with the top-level project requirements.
    \item \textbf{Concepts Considered Further (CCF) (Orange):} Options which could potentially work, yet appear to be unnecessarily complex in relation to alternatives, or it is not possible to analyse their feasibility within the time-frame of this project. These options were considered but not developed further into strawman concepts.
    \item \textbf{Accepted Concepts (Green):} Options deemed feasible and directly employed towards strawman concept development.
\end{itemize}

\section{Generation of Preliminary Configuration Concepts}
\label{sec:prelim_config_concepts}

Now that the design option tree has been screened to only allow for plausible subsystem options, potential aircraft configurations must be generated for holistic evaluation. If every accepted option in the DOT were combined simultaneously, it would result in an impractical number of potential aircraft concepts. For this reason, the configuration selection process is split up into 2 stages to make the selection procedure more manageable. Subsystems such as avionics, materials, and specific tail geometries were omitted from the configuration generation at this stage of the project since they are agnostic to the overall aerodynamic and structural configuration of the aircraft.

Stage 1 focuses exclusively on defining the optimal level of modularity for the aircraft. Because one of the core objectives of the flying laboratory is to test highly flexible aircraft components, the degree of modularity drives the structural weight, internal volume constraints, and overall feasibility of the testbed. Once the ideal modularity baseline is established in Stage 1, Stage 2 will integrate these findings with the remaining aerodynamic, structural and propulsion subsystems to generate comprehensive aircraft configurations for the final trade-off.

\subsection{Stage 1: Modularity Configuration Selection}
 The focus of this stage was strictly limited to the modularity branches of the design option tree: Wing Modularity, Canard Modularity, and Tail Modularity. Options that presented no distinct benefit, as well as CCFs, were previously pruned, leaving only the highly viable structural interfaces presented in \autoref{tab:modularity_design_options}.

From these modularity configurations, HUG-CFG-101, HUG-CFG-103, HUG-CFG-105, HUG-CFG-107 are not feasible due to a lack of tail adaptability. \subsection{Stage 2: Aerodynamic Layout and Integration}
\label{sec:stage_2_integration}

Stage 2 of the configuration selection focusses on the rest of the options of the tree which drive the design. Since the fuselage and skin materials cannot be decided upon at this stage, and branches such as propulsion mounting, location and undercarriage mounting location have been narrowed down to one option each, they need not be considered here. Additionally the tail configuration no longer needs to be decided, since all the modularity configurations include the ability to swap the tail section to whichever configuration is most suitable for the mission.  

As a result of this pre-screening, the driving parameters were successfully isolated to just two categories: Wing Vertical Position and Undercarriage type.

From these, some options can be pruned out for infeasibility or due to lack of merit. HUG-CFG-202 was rejected since low mounted wings are incompatible with skid landing, due to the inevitability of wing strikes during landing, especially considering the aircraft could be configured with high aspect ratio, flexible wings. Additionally, mid-wing configurations were eliminated because the required structural carry-through box obstructs the continuous internal fuselage volume needed for the payload, while simultaneously making the integration of a structurally sound, variable-wing mount much more difficult to integrate. Since fuselage mounted propulsion is the only remaining option in the tree, a high wing concept would result in the engines ingesting the wake from the wings, diminishing their performance. This leaves us with only one option from stage 2, namely, HUG-CFG-201.

If every accepted option in the DOT were combined, it would result in thousands of potential aircraft concepts, which would be impractical to evaluate. In order to narrow down the subsystems being evaluated, the configurations were built without the architecture-independent features.

In order to narrow down the 20 configurations to a shortlist, first, several architectural branches were systematically eliminated due to inherent conflicts with the testbed's primary mission:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mid-Wing Configurations (HUG-CFG-14, HUG-CFG-15)} Eliminated primarily due to internal packaging constraints. The structural carry-through box of a mid-wing design heavily obstructs the continuous internal volume required for the payload, as stated in  \textbf{HUG-STR-017}. Furthermore, it does not offer distinct aerodynamic advantages over a high or low-wing configuration to justify the harder structural integration and the necessary reinforcements\cite{raymer1992aircraft}.
    
    \item \textbf{Canard Configurations (HUG-CFG-07, HUG-CFG-15)} Discarded due to aerodynamic interference (\textbf{HUG-AER-004}).\cite{lopes2005canard} A forward canard generates downwash and a turbulent wake that affects the main wing. Because the core objective of this flying laboratory is to capture clean aeroelastic and flutter data from the main wing, upstream flow disturbances are unacceptable. Additionally, canards are less representative of the future commercial transport architectures this project aims to emulate.
    
    \item \textbf{High-Wing with Fuselage-Mounted Propulsion (HUG-CFG-16, HUG-CFG-17, HUG-CFG-19)} Eliminated due to aerodynamic and structural integration challenges. If the turbine is mounted on the aft fuselage sides, the intakes risk ingesting the turbulent wake generated by the high wing, degrading engine performance. Additionally, an engine placed above the fuselage introduces structural interference with the high wing's carry-through box and modular mounting mechanisms\cite{raymer1992aircraft}. While physically feasible, these conflicts make the high-wing architecture fundamentally inferior to the low-wing alternatives for this specific propulsion setup.
    
    \item \textbf{Wing-Mounted Propulsion (HUG-CFG-9, HUG-CFG-18, HUG-CFG-20)} Eliminated due to strict versatility and aeroelastic constraints. The drone is required to facilitate the testing of highly flexible, swappable HAR wings. The wings are considered the test structure, as such, mounting engines on them would introduce inertial and torsional coupling that would interfere with the aeroelastic data. This design also contradicts the modularity requirement(\textbf{HUG-AER-003}).
    
    %Hard-mounting a jet engine directly to these flexible test articles introduces massive inertial and torsional coupling that would corrupt the baseline flutter data, while simultaneously violating the requirement for wing modularity and versatility.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Identification of Concepts}
Many of the remaining configurations share fundamental aerodynamic and structural characteristics, allowing them to be grouped into families based on similarities. To avoid redundant analysis during the detailed trade-off phase, these families were evaluated to identify a few optimal representative configurations for each. This down-selection process effectively narrowed the field from 20 permutations to the following candidate architectures:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HUG-CFG-01 (Low Wing, Variable Tail Mounting, Retractable Gear):} Selected to serve as a versatile baseline model. This configuration can closely mirror standard transport aircraft designs while being adaptable to esoteric designs as well. Therefore, the aeroelastic and flutter data collected will be highly relevant and scalable to the commercial planes the testbed is designed to emulate. This configuration can also allow for the testing of an experimental wing at the canard mounting position while retaining  
    
    \item \textbf{HUG-CFG-08 (Low Wing, Inverted V-Tail, Retractable Gear):} This architecture was selected primarily for its clean upper aerodynamic surface. The inverted V-tail keeps the empennage clear of the upper fuselage, providing an unobstructed region to mount a test airfoil. This allows experimental wings to be tested while retaining a stable and optimized wing planform.
    
    \item \textbf{HUG-CFG-10 \& HUG-CFG-11 (Flying Wing, Buried Engine):} Selected for their exceptionally low parasitic drag profile \cite[p.~74]{raymer1992aircraft}. This high aerodynamic efficiency ensures the aircraft can easily and reliably reach the high Mach numbers necessary to trigger aeroelastic phenomena during flight testing. Both landing gear variants (retractable gear in Config 10 and skids in Config 11) are retained for further evaluation in the mid-term trade-off.
\end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Wing Mounted Only (Rejected):} The requirement to test "highly flexible, High-Aspect-Ratio (HAR) wings" dictates slender wing profiles lacking the internal volume to house the required fuel while leaving room for actuators.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Propulsion Group}

\paragraph{Mounting Location}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fuselage Mounted (Accepted):} Simplifies structure, centralises mass and allows for wing modularity.
    \item \textbf{Buried Engine (Accepted):} Aerodynamically efficient, minimizing drag for high subsonic speeds, but complicates modularity, cooling, and maintenance access.
    \item \textbf{Wing Mounted (Accepted):} Feasible, but mounting a turbine on a wing designed to be highly flexible and prone to flutter introduces severe inertial and structural coupling challenges. Additionally, it complicates the modularity requirement of the drone, since swapping the wing would also require swapping the engines.

\section{Constructing the Design Tree}

The Design Option Tree (DOT) is a method used to help generate and evaluate different design options. The first step in building the DOT was defining the top-level system, which is the complete aircraft. This was then decomposed into its primary subsystems: fuselage, empennage, wing, propulsion, and undercarriage. These subsystems represent the main functional divisions of the aircraft and form the first level of branching. Each subsystem was further broken down into key design aspects that significantly influence performance and feasibility. The selection of branches at each level was guided by design impact and the need to compare meaningful alternatives. All options that could lead to distinct design configurations were included in the tree.

The next step was rejecting the unfeasible options (in red) or marking them as straw-man concepts (in orange), while justifying the remaining acceptable concepts (in green).

\subsection{Rejected Design Concepts}

As part of the preliminary design process, several configuration options were evaluated using the Design Option Tree. Concepts that were found to be infeasible, incompatible with the design requirements, or overly complex were discarded at an early stage. The main reasons for rejection are summarized below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Internal structure material (polymer structures):} Discarded due to insufficient strength and stiffness to weight ratio for primary load-bearing applications.

    \item \textbf{Manual control only:} Requires continuous pilot or remote input across all flight phases, leading to increased workload and reduced operational robustness. Since basic autopilot, loitering and return to home are now standard in most flight control softwares, it makes no sense to exclude some level of autopilot capabilities in the design.

    \item \textbf{Twin-boom wing configuration:} Rejected due to increased drag, higher structural weight and added complexity.

    \item \textbf{Delta wing configuration:} Limits modularity and makes testing of high-aspect-ratio (HAR) wings impractical.

    \item \textbf{Prandtl infinite wing:} Discarded due to challenges in yaw control, sensitivity to flutter, and reduced effective lift generation.

    \item \textbf{Jet engine with auxiliary power unit:} An auxiliary power unit was considered to cover the electrical power requirements. \subsection{Straw-Man Design Concepts}

In addition to the rejected concepts, several design options were identified as straw-man concepts. These options are not immediately discarded but are considered less favorable due to performance limitations, integration challenges, or misalignment with key design objectives. They may still be feasible under certain conditions but are not prioritized in the current design phase.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Skin material (polymer):} Considered a straw-man due to potential thermal limitations, particularly in regions exposed to elevated temperatures (e.g., near fuselage-mounted engines), and possible fatigue concerns. However, it remains attractive in terms of manufacturability and rapid production.

    \item \textbf{Autopilot only:} A fully autonomous control system lacks flexibility, as external input is required to modify flight regimes (e.g., varying Mach numbers during testing). While feasible, it is not preferred as a standalone solution.

    \item \textbf{Engine-driven power generator:} Feasibility is uncertain due to potential integration challenges with the selected engine, and therefore requires further investigation.

    \item \textbf{V-tail configuration:} Offers no significant performance advantage while introducing increased control complexity, susceptibility to flutter, and possible adverse roll effects. However, it may provide integration benefits, such as enabling fuselage-mounted engine placement.

    \item \textbf{Non-quick-swappable wings:} Strongly limits modularity, making the testing of multiple wing configurations impractical. However, it simplifies integration by allowing permanent incorporation of fuel tanks, engines, and landing gear within the wings.

    \item \textbf{Wing-mounted engines:} Complicates modular wing concepts and introduces risks in scenarios such as belly landings. However, it may be required for specific test cases involving engine-induced loads, vibrations, and structural interactions.
    
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Accepted Design Concepts}

The following design concepts were accepted for further consideration in the preliminary design phase. These options were retained as they are feasible, align with aerospace design practice, or provide sufficient flexibility for later optimisation.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Internal structure material (composites and sheet metal):} Widely used in the aerospace industry, with established certification and operational reliability. These materials provide excellent load-bearing capability and structural performance.

    \item \textbf{Skin material (composites and sheet metal):} Selected based on the same justification as the internal structure materials, offering a good balance between strength, weight, and manufacturability.

    \item \textbf{Autopilot and manual control:} Combines the operational robustness and stability of autopilot systems with the flexibility of manual or remote input for adjusting flight regimes during testing.

    \item \textbf{Battery-powered subsystems:} Preferred due to their simplicity, ease of integration, and reduced system complexity compared to onboard power generation solutions.

    \item \textbf{Tail configurations:} All remaining tail configuration options are retained, as no early-stage constraints justify excluding any specific layout.

    \item \textbf{Wing configuration:} All viable wing configurations are retained, as no early-stage constraints justify excluding any specific layout.

    \item \textbf{Fuel tanks (fuselage-mounted and combined):} Enables modular wing design by removing the requirement for fuel storage within the wings. This enables quick-swappable wings, improves aerodynamic optimisation potential, and simplifies refuelling and maintenance through centralized fuel storage.

    \item \textbf{Number of engines (1 or 2):} Both single and twin-engine configurations remain feasible, depending on final engine specifications and performance requirements.

    \item \textbf{Undercarriage mounting points:} All potential mounting locations are currently considered feasible and remain open for further evaluation.

    \item \textbf{Undercarriage structure (retractable and skids):} Both configurations are retained. Retractable landing gear reduces drag at higher test speeds (up to Mach 0.75), while skid-based systems enable belly landings and reduce system weight by eliminating the need for landing gear.
\end{itemize}

\section{Preliminary configurations}
For the preliminary configuration selection, the Design Option Tree was deliberately simplified to focus on the most influential architectural choices: wing configuration, empennage configuration, propulsion mounting, and undercarriage mounting. This reduction was made because, at the preliminary design stage, the objective is to explore a manageable set of fundamentally different aircraft layouts rather than exhaustively analyze every subsystem detail. These four categories were selected as they have the greatest impact on the overall aircraft configuration and feasibility. By narrowing the scope of the DOT, the number of possible design combinations is significantly reduced, allowing for a more efficient comparison of configurations. More detailed aspects, such as material selection or control system design, are deferred to later stages once a baseline configuration has been identified. 

A total of 20 preliminary design configurations were selected for future evaluation. Most configurations adopt either a low- or high-wing arrangement, as integrating a mid-wing is considerably more complex. A mid-wing requires the structure to pass deeply through the fuselage, necessitating additional reinforcement and significantly reducing available internal space. Despite these challenges, mid-wing designs can effectively reduce interference drag and improve aerodynamic efficiency. Additionally, the low-wing configurations allow for shorter, lighter landing gear and facilitate easier engine and structural maintenance.

Several configuration combinations were also deemed unfeasible and excluded from consideration. For example, pairing a conventional tail with a fuselage-mounted engine was rejected because the tail would remain in the engine wake. Similarly, wing-mounted engines combined with skid landing gear raised concerns about engine strikes during landing. Moreover, the wing-mounted engines are not the preferred option due to the limitation they impose on quick-swappable wings and modularity. Finally, buried engine layouts were excluded for all configurations except the flying wing, due to the associated integration and structural complexities.
