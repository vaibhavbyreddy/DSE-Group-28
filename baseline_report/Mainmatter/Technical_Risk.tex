\chapter{Technical Risk Assessment}
According to EASA risk-based classification rules\footnote{\href{https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/drones-air-mobility/operating-drone/specific-category-civil-drones}{EASA Specific Category — Civil Drones} (accessed on 23.04.2026)}, the HUGO falls under the "specific" category of UAS (Unmanned Aircraft Systems). This classification follows from the design MTOM exceeding 25kg, maximum operational velocity of Mach 0.75, maximum altitude exceeding 120m, and BVLOS (Beyond Visual Line of Sight). To ensure operational safety, a preliminary risk assessment has to be performed for both development and operational risks. A qualitative assessment of risks is performed in \autoref{sec:risk_assessment}. Mitigation measures are identified and quantified in \autoref{sec:contingency_management}. Lastly, a preliminary Specific Operation Risk Assessment (SORA) methodology is followed in \autoref{sec:SORA} to determine the necessary measures required to ensure safe operations of HUGO.

\section{Initial Risk Assessment}
\label{sec:risk_assessment}

In risk assessment, key risks are identified by evaluating the likelihood of occurrence $p$ and severity of impact. For the purpose of this analysis, both of these criteria are evaluated on a scale from 1 to 5, with 1 being the lowest score and 5 being the highest. Details regarding the allocation of both scores are explained in \autoref{tab:risk_map_scores}. 

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
        \caption{Likelihood/Severity Scores used in Risk Assessment}
    \begin{tabular}{|p{1cm}|p{3.5cm}|p{10.5cm}|}
    \hline
        \textbf{Score} & \textbf{Likelihood} & \textbf{Severity} \\ \hline\hline
         1 & $0\%< p<1\%$ & Negligible \\   \hline
         2 & $1\%<p<30\%$ & Marginal (insignificant impact, can be managed by applying standard procedures) \\   \hline
         3 & $30\%<p<50\%$& Considerable (significant impact, can be managed by additional/contingency procedures) \\   \hline
         4 & $50\%<p<70\%$& Critical (possible injury or minor damage to property or HUGO, has to be addressed by contingency/emergency procedures) \\   \hline
         5 & $70\%<p<100\%$& Catastrophic (possible death or serious injury, severe damage to property, loss of HUGO, has to be addressed by contingency/emergency procedures) \\   \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:risk_map_scores}
\end{table}

\subsection{Development Risks}
For any engineering project it is vital to identify the risks that exist related to the development process. Finding the development risks for a project requires clearly defined requirements that this system must meet. Then identify everything that might prevent the final designed system from meeting these requirements. These risks can be defined by their severity (the risk they pose for the product to meet the requirements) and the probability of this occurring. \subsection{Operation risks}
Operation risks refer to hazardous scenarios which can occur both on the ground and in the air. They are identified and classified based on the following criteria \cite{Jarus_main}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The extent to which third parties or property on the ground could be endangered by the activity;
    \item The complexity, performance and operational characteristics of the unmanned aircraft involved;
    \item The purpose of the flight, UAS type, probability of collision with other aircraft, class of airspace;
    \item The type, scale, and complexity of the UAS operation or activity, including, where relevant, the size and type of the traffic handled by the responsible organisation or person;
    \item The extent to which the persons affected by the risks involved in the UAS operation are able to assess and exercise control over those risks.
\end{itemize}

Preventive measures are identified as process which decrease the likelihood of a risk occurring in the first place, while mitigation strategies are expected to lower the severity of consequences in case a risk manifests during development or operations.

    \item Monitoring the condition of critical components. This decrease the probability of \textbf{HUG-OP-04} and \textbf{HUG-OP-09}     (preventive). 
    \item Short service cycles on critical components. This decrease the probability of \textbf{HUG-OP-04} and \textbf{HUG-OP-09} (preventive).
    \item If the test area is exceeded, the drone will be ordered to immediately return to the testing location \textbf{HUG-OP-06} (contingency).
    \item In the case of a mechanical error onboard the drone during the test flight is detected, an analysis of the level of FUCKERY  will be assessed, and one of the 2 scenarios will be implemented: 1. The drone will be ordered to immediately return. 2. The first option will be implemented, and the drone will be lost. This decreases the impact \textbf{HUG-OP-09} (contingency).
    \item In case of deteriorating weather conditions, the drone will be ordered to return. This decreases the impact \textbf{HUG-OP-10} (contengency).

\section{Preliminary Specific Operation Risk Assessment (SORA)}
\label{sec:SORA}

The SORA is a classification method used by JARUS (Joint Authorities for Rulemaking on Unmanned Systems) to determine the risk posed by a UAS during operations \cite{Jarus_main}. Its main purpose is to determine whether the operations can be performed in a safe manner. The nominal SORA method is typically comprised of 10 steps encompassing the ground risk, air risk, mitigation performance requirements, assurance and integrity level estimation, and determination of containment requirements.

In this Section, a preliminary SORA classification will be performed for HUGO based on currently available data. It is expected that the final results of this methodology may change as more information becomes known about the UAS configuration and the mission area.

\subsection{Documentation of Proposed Operations}
The first step in the SORA methodology focuses on compiling operational, technical, and organisational information regarding UAS operations. This entails detailing the operational volume, ground risk buffers, adjacent area, and adjacent airspace \cite{Jarus_main}\cite{Jarus_Annex_A}.

For the remaining steps of the SORA, it is assumed that HUGO will operate in controlled areas only. This assumption will impact the determination of ground risk, assurance and integrity levels, and operational objectives.


\subsection{Intrinsic Ground Risk Class (iGRC)}
The intrinsic Ground Risk Class is determined by the characteristics of the UAS based on its maximum characteristic dimension (wing span), maximum speed, as well as the population density in the operational volume and risk buffer areas \cite{Jarus_main}. It is expressed by a number from 1 to 10, with higher values corresponding to higher ground risks.

The maximum speed of HUGO exceeds the limit of 200 m/s specified in the JARUS SORA Guide \cite{Jarus_main}. This implies that the iGRC has to be estimated according to alternative estimation methods defined in Annex F \cite{Jarus_Annex_F}. One of the available trade-offs allows for an increase in maximum velocity by 40\%, provided that the maximum size is reduced by 50\%. As the expected wingspan for HUGO falls below 20 m, this trade-off is applicable and can be used to estimate the iGRC. Table \autoref{tab:alternative_igrc_that_makes_us_legal} presents an alternative iGRC classification method applicable for the trade-offs.

\subsection{Final Ground Risk Class (GRC)}
If the iGRC value is too high, it can be modified or lowered based on risk mitigation scenarios described in the JARUS SORA Guide \cite{Jarus_main}. However, an iGRC of 3 is considered low enough for the purpose of classification of HUGO, and therefore the iGRC and GRC both attain a value of 3.



\subsection{Air Risk Class (ARC) and Tactical Mitigation Performance Requirement (TMPR)}
The SORA methodology uses the operational airspace of the UAS to determine an intrinsic risk of collision with (manned) aircraft, which is expressed by the ARC \cite{Jarus_main}. For a UAS operating in atypical airspaces (such as reserved/restricted airspaces or defined drone testing areas), such as HUGO, an ARC-a score is automatically attributed.

The ARC can be lowered or modified via strategic and tactical mitigation means \cite{Jarus_Annex_F}. However, since ARC-a represents the lowest possible ARC score, such steps are not deemed necessary for HUGO.

The TMPR refers to operational requirements which shall be applied to mitigate residual risks of air collisions with (manned) aircraft and achieve an acceptable level of safety during UAS operations \cite{Jarus_main}. For ARC-a operations, the risk of encounter of manned aircraft is deemed as "extremely low", and therefore no TMPR shall be applied (although it is possible for the CAA to require a demonstration of mitigation procedures for HUGO if deemed necessary)\cite{Jarus_Annex_A}\cite{Jarus_Annex_D}.

The SAIL is a value ranging from I to VI used for the identification of Operational Safety Objectives (OSOs) related to the robustness (integrity and assurance) level of the UAS \cite{Jarus_main}. Qualitatively, this metric represents the confidence in the safety of operations, and can be determined according to \autoref{tab:SAIL_determination_table}. \subsection{Comprehensive Safety Portfolio (CSP)}
The Comprehensive Safety Portfolio (CSP) compiles all elements of the SORA which shall be presented to the competent authority for the application of authorisation for UAS operations \cite{Jarus_Annex_F}. Typically, it includes the finalized detailed operational description, all safety claims and their robustness (iGRC and final GRC, initial and residual ARC, TMPR, the OSOs associated with the SAIL, and the containment requirements), compliance evidence, necessary linkages and references between documents, and a finalised compliance matrix.

At the current development stage of HUGO, it is not yet possible to compile a complete version of the CSP.
